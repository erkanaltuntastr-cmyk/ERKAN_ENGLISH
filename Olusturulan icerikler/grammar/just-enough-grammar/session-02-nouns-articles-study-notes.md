# Session 02 - Nouns, Articles, Countability, and Work-Order Vocabulary

## 1. Purpose of this session

This session is the first real grammar reset session after the baseline correction. The focus is nouns. This is not only a school grammar topic. For adult work English, nouns are the building blocks of almost every sentence you need for sales, customers, restaurants, products, prices, orders, payments, delivery, and relationships.

A sentence becomes much easier to control when you can identify the nouns first. Once the nouns are clear, you can decide whether each one needs `a`, `an`, `the`, no article, singular form, plural form, possessive form, or a preposition before it.

For your English, nouns matter because many of your baseline mistakes were not caused by advanced grammar. They were caused by missing or unnatural noun phrases:

- `food service sector` should become `the food service sector`.
- `regular order system` should become `a regular ordering system` or `a regular weekly ordering pattern`, depending on the meaning.
- `customers mostly Turkish restaurants` should become `Most of my customers are Turkish restaurants`.
- `gives regular weekly to me` should become `places a regular weekly order with me`.
- `salary and commisions on monthly payments` should become `a salary and commission based on monthly payments`.

These examples show the real purpose of this session. You are not learning nouns as an abstract grammar topic. You are learning how to build stable English noun phrases that you can use at work.

## 2. What a noun is

A noun names something. That something can be a person, a place, a physical thing, a group, an idea, a quality, an activity, or a business concept.

In everyday work English, nouns answer questions like:

- Who is involved? `customer`, `owner`, `manager`, `driver`, `supplier`, `salesperson`.
- What business is involved? `restaurant`, `cafe`, `shop`, `warehouse`, `supplier`, `company`.
- What product or service is involved? `olive oil`, `cheese`, `bread`, `delivery`, `invoice`, `price list`.
- What idea or business condition is involved? `quality`, `trust`, `relationship`, `commission`, `payment`, `margin`, `profit`, `discount`.
- What pattern or system is involved? `weekly order`, `ordering system`, `delivery schedule`, `payment terms`, `customer account`.

The most important practical rule is this: before you try to make a sentence more advanced, make sure the nouns are correct. If the noun phrase is broken, the whole sentence feels broken even if the main message is understandable.

A weak noun phrase:

`I work in food service sector.`

A stable noun phrase:

`I work in the food service sector.`

A more natural work sentence:

`I work in the food service sector, mainly with restaurants.`

The sentence did not need complicated grammar. It needed a correct noun phrase.

## 3. Noun phrases

A noun does not always stand alone. Very often it sits inside a noun phrase. A noun phrase is a noun plus the words that belong with it.

Examples:

- `customer`
- `a customer`
- `the customer`
- `my customer`
- `a regular customer`
- `one of my regular customers`
- `a regular Turkish restaurant customer`
- `a customer who places a weekly order`

The core noun is `customer`. The other words tell us which customer, what kind of customer, how many customers, or what relationship the customer has with you.

For your work English, it is better to learn complete noun phrases than isolated nouns. The word `order` is useful, but the phrase `a regular weekly order` is much more useful. The word `payment` is useful, but the phrase `monthly payments` or `payment terms` is more useful.

Important work noun phrases:

- `a regular weekly order`
- `the weekly order`
- `a new customer`
- `one of my regular customers`
- `a restaurant owner`
- `the restaurant owner`
- `a delivery issue`
- `the delivery time`
- `a price increase`
- `the price list`
- `a product problem`
- `the product quality`
- `a customer relationship`
- `the monthly payment`
- `payment terms`
- `a regular ordering system`
- `a better ordering habit`
- `a stable business relationship`

When you speak, do not build everything word by word from Turkish. Use ready noun phrases.

## 4. Types of nouns

### 4.1 Common nouns

Common nouns name general people, places, things, or ideas. They are not names of specific people, companies, cities, or brands.

Examples:

- `restaurant`
- `customer`
- `owner`
- `order`
- `delivery`
- `product`
- `price`
- `invoice`
- `payment`
- `quality`
- `relationship`

Common nouns usually need grammar decisions: singular or plural, countable or uncountable, article or no article.

Examples:

- `I visited a restaurant yesterday.`
- `The restaurant placed an order this morning.`
- `Most of my customers are restaurants.`
- `Product quality is very important.`

Notice the difference between `a restaurant`, `the restaurant`, and `restaurants`. The noun is similar, but the meaning changes.

### 4.2 Proper nouns

Proper nouns are names of specific people, places, organisations, brands, or titles. They normally begin with a capital letter.

Examples:

- `Erkan`
- `London`
- `the UK`
- `North London`
- `Star Catering`
- `Eversley Primary School`
- `Samsung`
- `Cambridge`

Some proper nouns use `the`; some do not. This is a major source of mistakes.

Examples:

- `I live in London.` No article before `London`.
- `I live in the UK.` Use `the` before `UK`.
- `I work with restaurants in North London.` No article before `North London`.
- `The United Kingdom is a proper noun phrase with the article built in.`

Practical rule: country names usually do not take `the`, but names that include words like `Kingdom`, `States`, `Republic`, or plural political names often use `the`.

Useful examples:

- `Turkey`
- `Britain`
- `England`
- `the UK`
- `the United Kingdom`
- `the United States`
- `the Netherlands`

### 4.3 Concrete nouns

Concrete nouns name things you can see, touch, taste, hear, or smell.

Examples from work:

- `box`
- `cheese`
- `bread`
- `olive oil`
- `yoghurt`
- `invoice`
- `van`
- `warehouse`
- `menu`
- `product sample`

Concrete nouns are useful in sales because customers often talk about visible or physical things:

- `The boxes arrived late.`
- `The cheese was damaged.`
- `The invoice is missing.`
- `The driver left the delivery outside.`

### 4.4 Abstract nouns

Abstract nouns name ideas, feelings, qualities, states, or business concepts. You cannot touch them directly, but they are very important in work English.

Examples:

- `quality`
- `trust`
- `reliability`
- `relationship`
- `profit`
- `margin`
- `commission`
- `confidence`
- `support`
- `service`
- `consistency`
- `communication`

Abstract nouns are often uncountable, but not always. This means they often appear with no article when used generally.

Examples:

- `Trust is important in sales.`
- `Quality matters more than price for some customers.`
- `Good communication helps us avoid mistakes.`
- `Consistency is important if we want regular orders.`

But some abstract nouns can become countable in certain meanings:

- `We have a good relationship with that customer.`
- `There was a problem with the delivery.`
- `I had a conversation with the owner.`
- `We need a solution before Friday.`

### 4.5 Collective nouns

Collective nouns name a group as one unit.

Examples:

- `team`
- `family`
- `staff`
- `management`
- `company`
- `group`
- `committee`

In British English, some collective nouns can be treated as singular or plural depending on whether you are thinking of the group as one unit or the people inside the group. For your current level, keep it simple and use singular verbs when the group is acting as one unit.

Examples:

- `The team is working on the order.`
- `The company is changing its price list.`
- `The management is reviewing the account.`

For natural everyday British English, you may hear plural use too:

- `The staff are very busy today.`

Do not overfocus on this now. The bigger priority is to build clear noun phrases and avoid missing verbs or articles.

### 4.6 Compound nouns

Compound nouns are made from two or more words that work together as one noun idea.

Work examples:

- `price list`
- `delivery time`
- `delivery slot`
- `product quality`
- `customer account`
- `restaurant owner`
- `payment terms`
- `sales target`
- `profit margin`
- `ordering system`
- `weekly order`
- `stock problem`
- `invoice number`
- `account manager`

Compound nouns are very important for you. Many natural business phrases are compound nouns. If you translate each word separately, your English becomes unnatural. Learn the compound phrase as one unit.

Less natural:

`regular order system`

Better:

`a regular ordering system`

More natural in a sales conversation:

`a regular weekly ordering pattern`

Less natural:

`price paper`

Better:

`price list`

Less natural:

`customer relation`

Better:

`customer relationship`

Less natural:

`monthly payment base`

Better:

`based on monthly payments`

## 5. Singular and plural nouns

A singular noun names one person, place, thing, idea, or unit. A plural noun names more than one.

Examples:

- `one restaurant` / `two restaurants`
- `one customer` / `many customers`
- `one order` / `several orders`
- `one invoice` / `three invoices`
- `one delivery` / `weekly deliveries`
- `one product` / `different products`

Plural nouns are not optional. If you mean more than one, the noun usually needs plural form.

Wrong or weak:

`Most of my customer are restaurant.`

Correct:

`Most of my customers are restaurants.`

More natural:

`Most of my customers are Turkish restaurants.`

### 5.1 Regular plural: add -s

Most English nouns become plural with `-s`.

Examples:

- `restaurant` -> `restaurants`
- `customer` -> `customers`
- `product` -> `products`
- `price` -> `prices`
- `order` -> `orders`
- `invoice` -> `invoices`
- `driver` -> `drivers`

Work examples:

- `I visit several restaurants every week.`
- `Some customers order more regularly than others.`
- `The new prices start next month.`
- `We had three delivery issues last week.`

### 5.2 Plural with -es

Some nouns add `-es`, especially nouns ending in sounds like `s`, `sh`, `ch`, `x`, or similar endings.

Examples:

- `box` -> `boxes`
- `dish` -> `dishes`
- `watch` -> `watches`
- `branch` -> `branches`

Work examples:

- `The boxes arrived damaged.`
- `The restaurant ordered new dishes for the menu.`
- `We supply several branches.`

### 5.3 Nouns ending in consonant + y

When a noun ends in consonant + `y`, change `y` to `ies`.

Examples:

- `delivery` -> `deliveries`
- `company` -> `companies`
- `category` -> `categories`

Work examples:

- `We had two late deliveries this week.`
- `Some companies pay monthly.`
- `The products are divided into different categories.`

But if the word ends in vowel + `y`, usually just add `s`:

- `day` -> `days`
- `key` -> `keys`

### 5.4 Irregular plurals

Some nouns do not follow a regular rule.

Examples:

- `person` -> `people`
- `man` -> `men`
- `woman` -> `women`
- `child` -> `children`
- `foot` -> `feet`
- `tooth` -> `teeth`

Work examples:

- `I speak to many people every week.`
- `The restaurant has three delivery men.`
- `The owner has two children.`

Do not try to master every irregular plural now. Start with the ones you actually use.

## 6. Countable and uncountable nouns

This is one of the most important practical areas for you.

A countable noun can be counted as separate units.

Examples:

- `a restaurant`, `two restaurants`
- `a customer`, `many customers`
- `an order`, `several orders`
- `an invoice`, `three invoices`
- `a delivery`, `weekly deliveries`

An uncountable noun is not normally counted as separate units in everyday grammar. It is treated like a mass, category, material, or general concept.

Examples:

- `food`
- `information`
- `advice`
- `work`
- `traffic`
- `equipment`
- `stock`
- `commission`
- `quality`
- `support`
- `service`
- `rice`
- `cheese`
- `olive oil`

You can still measure uncountable nouns, but you need another word:

- `a piece of advice`
- `some information`
- `a lot of traffic`
- `two boxes of cheese`
- `three bottles of olive oil`
- `a high level of service`
- `a small amount of commission`

### 6.1 Why countability changes your sentence

Countable singular nouns normally need a determiner such as `a`, `an`, `the`, `my`, `this`, or `one`.

Wrong:

`I want to create regular ordering system.`

Correct:

`I want to create a regular ordering system.`

Why? `system` is a singular countable noun. You cannot normally leave it alone.

Correct alternatives:

- `a regular ordering system`
- `the regular ordering system`
- `my regular ordering system`
- `this regular ordering system`
- `one regular ordering system`

Plural countable nouns can appear without an article when you speak generally.

Examples:

- `Restaurants need reliable suppliers.`
- `Customers want clear prices.`
- `Orders usually increase before busy periods.`

Uncountable nouns can also appear without an article when you speak generally.

Examples:

- `Food quality is important.`
- `Commission depends on monthly payments.`
- `Good service builds trust.`
- `Advice from experienced people can help.`

### 6.2 Common countable nouns in your work

Use these with `a/an` when singular.

- `a customer`
- `a restaurant`
- `an owner`
- `an order`
- `an invoice`
- `a delivery`
- `a product`
- `a price`
- `a discount`
- `a problem`
- `a solution`
- `a system`
- `a relationship`
- `a conversation`
- `a meeting`
- `a payment`
- `a margin`
- `a target`

Examples:

- `I had a conversation with the owner.`
- `There is a problem with the order.`
- `We need a solution before the next delivery.`
- `This customer has a good weekly ordering habit.`

### 6.3 Common uncountable nouns in your work

Do not use `a` directly before these when they are used generally.

- `food`
- `stock`
- `equipment`
- `information`
- `advice`
- `support`
- `service`
- `quality`
- `trust`
- `commission`
- `profit` in general use
- `training`
- `traffic`
- `feedback`

Examples:

- `I need more information about the order.`
- `Good service is important for regular customers.`
- `Product quality is one of our strengths.`
- `I earn commission based on monthly payments.`
- `There was too much traffic this morning.`

Be careful: some words can be both countable and uncountable depending on meaning.

`business`:

- `Business is slow today.` General activity; uncountable.
- `He owns a business.` One company; countable.

`coffee`:

- `I drink coffee.` General drink; uncountable.
- `Can I have a coffee?` One cup; countable in everyday speech.

`service`:

- `Good service matters.` General quality; uncountable.
- `We offer a delivery service.` A specific type of service; countable.

## 7. Articles: a, an, the, and zero article

Articles are small words, but they make English sound complete. Turkish does not work in the same way, so article mistakes are normal. But in your English, article control will make a big difference.

### 7.1 Use `a` or `an` for one general singular countable noun

Use `a` before a consonant sound.

Examples:

- `a customer`
- `a restaurant`
- `a product`
- `a delivery`
- `a problem`
- `a solution`
- `a system`
- `a regular weekly order`

Use `an` before a vowel sound.

Examples:

- `an order`
- `an invoice`
- `an owner`
- `an account`
- `an issue`
- `an example`
- `an opportunity`

Work examples:

- `I spoke to a customer this morning.`
- `A restaurant placed an order with me.`
- `We had an issue with the delivery.`
- `I sent an invoice to the customer.`

Important: `a/an` means one, but not a specific known one yet. You introduce something for the first time.

Example:

`A restaurant called me this morning. The restaurant wants to change its order.`

First mention: `a restaurant`. Second mention: `the restaurant`.

### 7.2 Use `the` when the noun is specific or already known

Use `the` when both speaker and listener know which one, or when the noun has already been introduced.

Examples:

- `the customer`
- `the restaurant`
- `the owner`
- `the order`
- `the delivery`
- `the invoice`
- `the price list`
- `the payment`
- `the UK`

Work examples:

- `The customer wants the delivery before 10 a.m.`
- `The owner asked for the new price list.`
- `The order was placed yesterday.`
- `The invoice has already been sent.`
- `I work in the food service sector in the UK.`

### 7.3 Use no article for general plural nouns

When you speak generally about plural countable nouns, you often use no article.

Examples:

- `Restaurants need reliable suppliers.`
- `Customers want clear prices.`
- `Owners do not like late deliveries.`
- `Invoices should be clear.`
- `Regular orders help build stable relationships.`

Do not say:

`The restaurants need reliable suppliers` if you mean restaurants in general.

Use `the restaurants` only if you mean a specific group:

`The restaurants in my area are very busy this week.`

### 7.4 Use no article for general uncountable nouns

When you speak generally about uncountable nouns, you normally use no article.

Examples:

- `Quality is important.`
- `Trust takes time.`
- `Commission depends on payment.`
- `Information is useful.`
- `Advice helps when it is practical.`
- `Traffic causes delivery problems.`

But use `the` when the uncountable noun is specific:

- `The quality of this product is very good.`
- `The information you sent me was useful.`
- `The traffic on the North Circular was terrible.`
- `The commission for this account is small.`

## 8. Nouns as sentence jobs

A noun can do different jobs in a sentence. The noun itself remains a noun, but its role changes.

### 8.1 Noun as subject

The subject is the person or thing the sentence is about. It often performs the action or carries the main state.

Examples:

- `The customer called me.`
- `The restaurant placed an order.`
- `The delivery arrived late.`
- `The invoice is ready.`
- `Product quality matters.`

How to find the subject:

Ask `Who?` or `What?` before the verb.

Sentence: `The customer called me.`

Question: `Who called me?`

Answer: `The customer.`

Sentence: `The delivery arrived late.`

Question: `What arrived late?`

Answer: `The delivery.`

This is important because English sentences normally need a subject and a verb.

Broken sentence:

`My customers mostly Turkish restaurants.`

Problem: There is a subject phrase, but there is no full verb.

Correct sentence:

`Most of my customers are Turkish restaurants.`

### 8.2 Noun as subject complement

A subject complement comes after a linking verb like `be`, and it renames or describes the subject.

Examples:

- `My main job is sales.`
- `The owner is my customer.`
- `This product is a bestseller.`
- `That restaurant is a regular account.`
- `Commission is part of my income.`

In these sentences, the verb does not show an action. It links the subject to more information.

Pattern:

`Subject + be + noun complement`

Examples:

- `My job is customer management.`
- `The main problem is delivery time.`
- `The best solution is a regular weekly order.`

### 8.3 Possessive nouns

A possessive noun shows ownership, relationship, or connection.

Use apostrophe + `s` with most singular nouns:

- `the customer's order`
- `the restaurant's account`
- `the owner's question`
- `the driver's route`
- `the company's price list`

Use only an apostrophe after regular plural nouns ending in `s`:

- `the customers' payments`
- `the restaurants' orders`
- `the drivers' schedules`

But with irregular plural nouns, use apostrophe + `s`:

- `people's opinions`
- `children's meals`
- `men's shoes`

Work examples:

- `The customer's order is ready.`
- `The restaurant's payment is late.`
- `The owner's decision is important.`
- `The customers' payments arrive at the end of the month.`

Practical warning: do not overuse possessive nouns when an `of` phrase sounds clearer.

Natural:

- `the quality of the product`
- `the price of the item`
- `the owner of the restaurant`
- `the manager of the branch`

Also natural:

- `the product quality`
- `the item price`
- `the restaurant owner`
- `the branch manager`

Choose the phrase that sounds natural in business English.

### 8.4 Noun as direct object

A direct object receives the action of the verb.

Examples:

- `The customer placed an order.`
- `I sent the invoice.`
- `The driver delivered the products.`
- `The owner checked the price list.`
- `I called the restaurant.`

Ask `what?` or `whom?` after the verb.

Sentence: `The customer placed an order.`

Question: `Placed what?`

Answer: `an order`.

Sentence: `I called the restaurant.`

Question: `Called whom/what?`

Answer: `the restaurant`.

This is useful because many natural work collocations include a verb plus a noun object.

Important collocations:

- `place an order`
- `send an invoice`
- `make a payment`
- `build a relationship`
- `solve a problem`
- `offer a discount`
- `arrange a delivery`
- `check the stock`
- `confirm the price`
- `update the price list`

### 8.5 Noun as object of a preposition

A noun can come after a preposition. Together, they create a prepositional phrase.

Examples:

- `with the customer`
- `to the restaurant`
- `from the supplier`
- `for the owner`
- `about the order`
- `on the invoice`
- `in the UK`
- `at the warehouse`
- `before the delivery`
- `after the payment`

Work examples:

- `I spoke with the customer.`
- `I sent the invoice to the restaurant.`
- `The products came from the supplier.`
- `I asked about the order.`
- `The information is on the invoice.`
- `I work in the UK.`

The noun after the preposition is not the subject. It is part of the prepositional phrase. This matters because the real subject and verb still need to be clear.

## 9. Work-English noun patterns to practise

### 9.1 Introducing your work

Core pattern:

`I work in + sector/industry/place.`

Examples:

- `I work in the food service sector.`
- `I work in sales.`
- `I work in restaurant supply.`
- `I work in the wholesale food sector.`

Expanded pattern:

`I work in the food service sector, mainly with Turkish restaurants.`

More natural version:

`I work in the food service sector, mainly with restaurant owners in London.`

### 9.2 Talking about customers

Core pattern:

`Most of my customers are + plural noun.`

Examples:

- `Most of my customers are Turkish restaurants.`
- `Most of my customers are independent restaurants.`
- `Most of my customers are regular accounts.`
- `Most of my customers are small business owners.`

Do not leave out `are`.

Wrong:

`My customers mostly Turkish restaurants.`

Correct:

`Most of my customers are Turkish restaurants.`

### 9.3 Talking about orders

Core pattern:

`A customer places an order with me.`

Examples:

- `A regular customer places an order with me every week.`
- `The restaurant placed an order yesterday.`
- `They usually place their weekly order on Monday.`
- `I want customers to place regular weekly orders.`

Avoid:

`The customer gives an order to me.`

Use:

`The customer places an order with me.`

### 9.4 Talking about payment and commission

Core patterns:

- `I earn a salary.`
- `I earn commission.`
- `My commission is based on monthly payments.`
- `The customer makes a payment.`
- `The payment is late.`
- `Monthly payments affect my commission.`

Examples:

- `I earn a salary and commission.`
- `My commission is based on their monthly payments.`
- `If the customer pays regularly, the account becomes more stable.`

### 9.5 Talking about product quality

Core patterns:

- `Product quality is important.`
- `The quality of this product is very good.`
- `The customer asked about the product quality.`
- `There was a quality issue with the last delivery.`

Examples:

- `Product quality is one of the main reasons customers stay with us.`
- `The quality of the cheese was good, but the delivery was late.`
- `We need to solve the quality issue before the next order.`

## 10. Your key mistakes to avoid in this session

### Mistake 1: Missing article before a singular countable noun

Wrong:

`I want to create regular ordering system.`

Correct:

`I want to create a regular ordering system.`

Reason: `system` is singular and countable.

### Mistake 2: Missing `the` with recognised sectors or defined nouns

Wrong:

`I work in food service sector in UK.`

Correct:

`I work in the food service sector in the UK.`

Reason: `the food service sector` and `the UK` are fixed/defined noun phrases.

### Mistake 3: Singular noun when plural meaning is needed

Wrong:

`Most of my customer are Turkish restaurant.`

Correct:

`Most of my customers are Turkish restaurants.`

Reason: `Most of my...` usually points to plural countable nouns.

### Mistake 4: Wrong collocation with `order`

Wrong:

`A restaurant gives an order to me.`

Correct:

`A restaurant places an order with me.`

Reason: Natural business English uses `place an order`.

### Mistake 5: Confusing noun phrase and full sentence

Wrong:

`My customers mostly Turkish restaurants.`

Correct:

`Most of my customers are Turkish restaurants.`

Reason: A full sentence needs a subject and a finite verb. Here the verb is `are`.
